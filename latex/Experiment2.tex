\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate SART on three synthetic reasoning benchmarks with controlled shortcut injection.

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

\paragraph{Model and Training.}
We use a GPT-style transformer (6 layers, $d_{\mathrm{model}}=512$, 8 heads, $d_{\mathrm{ff}}=2048$, $\approx$19.0M parameters). All models are trained for 50 epochs with AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled}, learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch size 128, and cosine annealing. For SART: $\alpha=\beta=1.0$, $\tau_A=0.3$, $\tau_R=0.5$, $\lambda=2.0$, $\gamma=0.8$, $\rho=0.7$.

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We construct three synthetic datasets with controlled shortcut injection, each with 10{,}000 train / 2{,}000 val / 3{,}000 test samples. Training labels are 70\% shortcut-rule and 30\% true-rule; validation and test always follow the true rule. The test set is split evenly into \emph{clean} and \emph{perturbed} (shortcut contradicts the correct answer).
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Math-Arithmetic}: Classify $a+b \geq 10$. Shortcut: $a \geq 5$; true rule: evaluate the full sum.
    \item \textbf{Financial-Analysis}: Determine regulatory compliance from four features. Shortcut: revenue $\geq 5$; true rule: margin $\geq 5$ and debt $< 5$.
    \item \textbf{Causal-Reasoning}: Determine causal direction from observed variables and a confounder. Shortcut: correlation $\geq 5$; true rule: $x \geq 5$ and $z < 3$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare SART against: (1)~\textbf{Standard Fine-Tuning (SFT)}: no shortcut mitigation; (2)~\textbf{Self-Consistency Decoding}~\cite{wang2023selfconsistency}: majority vote over 5 samples at temperature 0.8; (3)~\textbf{Data Filtering}: remove samples with answer-token confidence $> 0.90$ after a 3-epoch warmup, then retrain.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Clean accuracy, perturbed accuracy (robustness), reasoning consistency (fraction of traces matching ground-truth patterns), and shortcut detection F1.

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows that SART attains 74.7\% clean accuracy (+8.0~pp over SFT) while simultaneously improving perturbed accuracy to 36.2\% (+12.0~pp). Self-Consistency yields marginal gains (+0.6~pp clean, +0.5~pp perturbed), confirming that inference-time diversity cannot compensate for training-time bias. Data Filtering achieves higher robustness (51.6\%) but drops clean accuracy to 56.7\% ($-$10.0~pp). Per-dataset results (Table~\ref{tab:per_dataset}) reveal that on \textsc{Causal-Reasoning}, Data Filtering's anomalously high perturbed accuracy (76.0\%, exceeding its own clean accuracy) indicates shortcut inversion rather than genuine correction. SART avoids this pathology and achieves stable gains across all datasets.

We isolate each component's contribution (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}). Removing Reweighting costs 4.0~pp accuracy and 6.4~pp robustness; removing Gradient Surgery costs 3.3~pp and 4.7~pp respectively. The full method exceeds the sum of individual robustness gains (+7.4~pp and +5.6~pp individually, +12.0~pp combined), confirming synergistic interaction. Reweighting controls \emph{which} samples dominate the loss landscape; Gradient Surgery corrects the \emph{direction} of residual shortcut gradients. Neither alone is sufficient.

\subsection{ShortcutScore Validation}
\label{sec:exp_detection}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{results/figure3.png}
    \caption{Empirical validation of \textsc{ShortcutScore}. (a) Score vs.\ true shortcut rate (Pearson $r=0.67$). (b) Gradient alignment distribution: shortcut samples (red) skew negative; non-shortcut (green) cluster near zero. (c) Exponential reweighting with $\lambda=2.0$.}
    \label{fig:analysis}
\end{figure}

\textsc{ShortcutScore} achieves detection F1 = 0.87, compared to 0.59 for confidence-based filtering---a 47\% relative improvement. Figure~\ref{fig:analysis}(a) confirms a positive monotonic correlation between $S(s)$ and true shortcut rate ($r=0.67$). The gradient alignment distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis}b) shows clear separation: shortcut samples skew toward negative alignment, while non-shortcut samples cluster near zero. Confidence-based filtering fails because shortcut samples are correctly labeled and confidently predicted---indistinguishable from genuinely easy samples by output probability alone. This confirms that gradient structure is fundamentally more informative than output confidence for shortcut detection.

\subsection{Computational Cost}
\label{sec:exp_efficiency}

The full pipeline completes in under 2 minutes on a single NVIDIA H100 NVL GPU. SART incurs modest training overhead versus SFT, attributable to one-time per-sample gradient computation. Validation gradients are recomputed periodically rather than per-step, keeping overhead manageable. This cost can be further reduced via gradient checkpointing or random projections for larger models.
