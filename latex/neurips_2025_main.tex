\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) often rely on reasoning shortcuts---surface pattern matching, answer memorization, and keyword correlations---rather than genuine logical inference. This stems from training signal misalignment: shortcut-encouraging samples efficiently reduce loss but harm generalization under distribution shift. We formulate \textbf{Shortcut-Aware Reasoning Training (SART)}: a framework that identifies training samples promoting shortcut updates and modifies gradient dynamics to emphasize genuine reasoning signals. Our key insight is that shortcut samples produce distinct gradient signatures---low alignment with validation gradients and high concentration on answer tokens. We propose \textbf{ShortcutScore}, a composite metric combining these two signals for sample reweighting, and \textbf{gradient surgery}, which projects harmful gradient components onto orthogonal subspaces to neutralize shortcuts while preserving reasoning signals. Experiments on three reasoning benchmarks with a 3.2M-parameter transformer demonstrate that SART improves accuracy by +23.7\,pp and robustness by +68.5\,pp over standard fine-tuning, surpassing eleven baselines---including methods requiring group annotations or multiple environments---by +16.5\,pp accuracy and +40.2\,pp robustness over the strongest competitor. Ablation reveals dramatic super-additive synergy: individual component gains sum to 41.8\,pp robustness, yet the combination achieves +68.5\,pp (+26.7\,pp synergy bonus). Code: \url{https://github.com/fuyanjie/short-cut-aware-data-centric-reasoning}
\end{abstract}

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Language model training and reasoning.}
Current training paradigms predominantly optimize for answer correctness, inadvertently encouraging shortcut exploitation~\cite{Author_Year_KeyWord}. Chain-of-Thought training~\cite{wei2022chain,kojima2022large} encourages intermediate reasoning traces but does not modify training dynamics, allowing shortcuts to persist in learned parameters---models can generate plausible reasoning steps that still rely on shortcuts. Self-Consistency Decoding~\cite{wang2022self} samples multiple reasoning paths to detect inconsistencies at inference time but cannot correct shortcut patterns already encoded during training, leaving the core training process unchanged. Data Filtering and Curriculum Learning~\cite{bengio2009curriculum} remove noisy or difficult samples but miss correctly labeled shortcut samples that appear indistinguishable from high-quality data. RLHF~\cite{ouyang2022training} optimizes reward signals based on answer correctness, not reasoning validity, allowing shortcuts to persist as long as they produce correct answers on the training distribution. These approaches demonstrate the inability to jointly address shortcut detection from gradient behavior and dynamic suppression during training.
%
\textbf{Gradient-based optimization for robustness.}
Standard gradient methods cannot distinguish reasoning-improving gradients from shortcut-reinforcing ones~\cite{Author_Year_KeyWord}. Prior gradient modification techniques~\cite{Author_Year_KeyWord,yu2020gradient} improve general robustness but lack principled mechanisms to identify \emph{which} gradients are detrimental to reasoning and \emph{why}---they modify gradients uniformly without targeting shortcut-specific patterns. Training signal adjustment methods~\cite{Author_Year_KeyWord} do not incorporate gradient-based detection of shortcut samples and cannot quantify the ``shortcut-ness'' of a sample based on its gradient behavior. Our work uniquely combines gradient alignment and answer-gradient concentration into a unified ShortcutScore, enabling targeted detection and suppression of shortcut-promoting training signals---a more principled approach than uniform gradient modification or sample removal.


\section{Conclusion}

We presented SART, a gradient-aware training framework that detects and corrects shortcut reasoning through ShortcutScore-based sample reweighting and gradient surgery. Three key findings emerge from our experiments with a 3.2M-parameter transformer on three reasoning benchmarks.

First, SART achieves 92.5\% accuracy and 87.9\% robustness, improving over standard fine-tuning by +23.7\,pp and +68.5\,pp respectively, and surpassing eleven baselines---including methods requiring group annotations (Group DRO) or multiple environments (IRM, V-REx, Fishr)---by +16.5\,pp accuracy and +40.2\,pp robustness over the strongest competitor. Second, ablation analysis reveals dramatic super-additive synergy: individual robustness gains sum to 41.8\,pp, yet the combination achieves +68.5\,pp---a +26.7\,pp synergy bonus (64\% beyond the sum of parts). This arises because reweighting and surgery operate on complementary axes (sample importance vs.\ gradient direction). Third, an 80-configuration hyperparameter search identifies the effective operating regime: strong reweighting ($\lambda{=}3.0$), full gradient projection ($\gamma{=}1.0$), and moderate answer suppression ($\rho{=}0.5$).

SART operates purely on training dynamics, requires no architectural modifications, group annotations, or multiple environments, and incurs approximately $2.5{\times}$ overhead. Future work will explore adaptive combination strategies and extend SART to larger-scale pretrained models and diverse reasoning domains.
